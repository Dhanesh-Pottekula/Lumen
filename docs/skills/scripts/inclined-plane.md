# Visual Script — "The Inclined Plane: How a Ramp Splits Gravity"

**Runtime:** ~1:55. **Background:** clean blueprint grid (this is a forces lesson — the grid says
"we measure things here"). **Recurring anchor:** a block sitting on a ramp; we keep returning to it and
adding one force arrow at a time until the full free-body diagram is built. **Voice:** an experienced
physics teacher who *builds the diagram in front of you*, never dumping it all at once.

Every force is a colored arrow drawn ON the block, added one at a time and never removed until it's been
understood. Objects list **IN** (appear · where · how), behavior, **OUT** (removed · how).

---

## SCENE 1 — Why Does It Slide? (0:00 – 0:12)
**Goal:** pose the question with a demonstration, not words.
**Sees:** A **ramp** rises from left to bottom-right. A **block** rests near the top. It's released and
**slides down**, speeding up, and shoots off the bottom. We rewind and freeze it mid-ramp — "let's find
out why."
- **Ramp** — a right-triangle wedge, angle θ at the bottom-left. IN 0:00 (draws on). STAYS (the whole lesson).
- **Block** — a labeled box near the top of the ramp. IN 0:02 (drops onto ramp). slides down 0:04–0:08,
  then **rewinds** and freezes mid-ramp at 0:10. STAYS.
- **Title** "What makes it slide — and how fast?" — top. IN 0:05 (word-by-word). OUT 0:12.
- **Angle marker θ** — small arc at the ramp's base corner. IN 0:09. STAYS (referenced all lesson).
**VO:** "Let a block go on a ramp and it slides — faster and faster. Simple to watch, but *why*? And what
decides how quickly? To answer that, we have to look at the forces."

---

## SCENE 2 — Gravity Pulls Straight Down (0:12 – 0:26)
**Goal:** the only "real" force starting point — weight, straight down.
**Sees:** From the block's center, a bold **downward arrow (mg)** grows, pointing straight to the ground
— not along the ramp, *straight down*. A small dropped apple beside it falls vertically to reinforce
"down is down."
- **Weight arrow mg** — thick red, from block center straight down. IN 0:14 (grows down). STAYS to the
  full diagram (until 1:30-ish).
- **Label "mg (weight)"** — beside the arrow. IN 0:16. STAYS with the arrow.
- **Reference apple** — small, right side, falls straight down 0:20–0:23. OUT 0:24 (fades).
- **Callout** "Gravity always points straight down — never along the ramp." IN 0:21. OUT 0:26.
**VO:** "Gravity does only one thing: it pulls the block straight down, toward the center of the Earth.
Not along the ramp — straight down. That single force is where everything starts."

---

## SCENE 3 — The Trick: Split Gravity in Two (0:26 – 0:48)
**Goal:** THE key idea — decompose weight into along-ramp and into-ramp components.
**Sees:** The straight-down **mg** arrow **splits** into two new arrows at the block: one **parallel to
the ramp surface** pointing downhill (labeled **mg sinθ — the "slide" force**), and one **perpendicular,
pressing into the ramp** (labeled **mg cosθ**). Dashed lines complete a right-angle triangle showing mg
is the hypotenuse; the angle θ reappears inside it, revealing *why* sin and cos. The two component arrows
briefly "recombine" into mg and split again to cement it.
- **mg arrow** — (from Scene 2) stays, but at 0:28 it **dims to a ghost** so the components stand out.
- **Along-ramp component (mg sinθ)** — orange arrow down the slope from the block. IN 0:30 (grows).
  STAYS to 1:30.
- **Into-ramp component (mg cosθ)** — blue arrow perpendicular into the surface. IN 0:33 (grows). STAYS.
- **Decomposition triangle** — dashed right triangle with mg as hypotenuse, θ marked inside. IN 0:36
  (draws). OUT 0:46 (fades, its job done).
- **Labels "mg sinθ (slides it)" / "mg cosθ (presses it)"** — on each component. IN 0:38. STAYS with arrows.
- **Recombine flourish** — components merge to mg then re-split, 0:42–0:45.
**VO:** "Here's the move that unlocks every ramp problem: split gravity into two. One part slides the
block *down the slope* — that's mg sine theta. The other presses it *into the ramp* — mg cosine theta.
Steepness decides how big each part is."

---

## SCENE 4 — The Ramp Pushes Back (0:48 – 1:02)
**Goal:** the normal force cancels the into-ramp component.
**Sees:** From the ramp surface under the block, a **green arrow (N, normal force)** grows outward,
**exactly opposite and equal** to the blue mg cosθ. The two arrows briefly overlap and flash "balanced"
— the block doesn't sink into the ramp. mg cosθ and N annihilate visually into a small "= 0" tag
perpendicular to the surface.
- **Normal force N** — green arrow perpendicular out of the surface. IN 0:50 (grows). STAYS to 1:30.
- **Balance flash** — N and mg cosθ pulse together, a "N = mg cosθ" tag appears. IN 0:55. OUT 1:02.
- **Label "N (ramp pushes back)"** — by the green arrow. IN 0:52. STAYS.
**VO:** "The block doesn't fall *through* the ramp, so the surface must push back — the normal force,
green, straight out of the surface. It exactly cancels the pressing-in part. Perpendicular to the slope,
nothing's left. All the action is *along* the ramp."

---

## SCENE 5 — Steepness Changes Everything (1:02 – 1:22)
**Goal:** as θ grows, mg sinθ grows and mg cosθ shrinks — shown live and as a graph.
**Sees:** The ramp **tilts up live from shallow to steep**. As it does, the orange **mg sinθ arrow grows**
and the blue **mg cosθ arrow shrinks**, in real time. Beside it, a **graph** plots both: a **sinθ curve
rising** and a **cosθ curve falling**, **crossing at 45°**. Markers: at **0°** the slide force is zero
(nothing moves); at **90°** it's the full weight (free fall).
- **Tilting ramp + block + its two component arrows** — the ramp angle animates 1:02–1:14; arrows resize
  live. STAYS.
- **Force-vs-angle graph** — right side; sinθ (orange, rising) and cosθ (blue, falling) curves. IN 1:06
  (draws). STAYS to 1:22.
- **45° crossing marker** — where the curves meet, labeled "forces equal." IN 1:12. OUT 1:22.
- **End labels "0°: no slide" / "90°: full weight"** — at the graph's ends. IN 1:16. OUT 1:22.
**VO:** "Now tilt it. Steeper, and the sliding force grows while the pressing force fades. Flat, and
nothing slides. Vertical, and it's just free fall. At forty-five degrees they're exactly equal — the
whole behavior of every ramp lives in these two curves."

---

## SCENE 6 — Friction Fights Back (1:22 – 1:40)
**Goal:** friction opposes sliding; whether it moves is a contest.
**Sees:** Back to a fixed ramp. A **purple friction arrow (f)** grows *up the slope*, opposing mg sinθ.
A tug-of-war feel: the orange **mg sinθ** (down) vs purple **f** (up). Two ramps then appear side by
side — a **gentle one where f wins (block stays)** and a **steep one where mg sinθ wins (block slides)**.
- **Friction arrow f** — purple, up the slope on the block. IN 1:24 (grows). STAYS to 1:34.
- **Tug-of-war bracket** — links f (up) and mg sinθ (down) with a "vs". IN 1:27. OUT 1:34.
- **Two-ramp comparison** — LEFT gentle (block static, "f > mg sinθ · stays"); RIGHT steep (block slides,
  "mg sinθ > f · goes"). IN 1:34 (fade). OUT 1:40.
**VO:** "But surfaces grip. Friction, purple, pushes *up* the slope, fighting the slide. It's a contest:
if the sliding force beats friction, the block moves; if not, it stays put. Gentle ramp — friction wins.
Steep ramp — gravity wins."

---

## SCENE 7 — Net Force → Acceleration (1:40 – 1:56)
**Goal:** what's left over accelerates the block.
**Sees:** On the sliding case, the up-friction and part of the down mg sinθ **cancel**, leaving a single
**net arrow** down the slope. The block **slides and speeds up**, and a **speed readout climbs** while a
small **velocity-vs-time line rises** beside it — net force means steady acceleration.
- **Net force arrow** — the leftover of mg sinθ − f, down the slope, bold. IN 1:42 (the two arrows
  subtract into it). STAYS to 1:52.
- **Block sliding** — accelerates down the ramp 1:44–1:52.
- **Speed readout (m/s)** — climbs with the motion, top corner. IN 1:44. STAYS to 1:56.
- **v–t line** — small graph, straight rising line. IN 1:47 (draws). STAYS to 1:56.
**VO:** "Whatever's left after friction is the net force — and net force means acceleration. The block
doesn't just slide, it *speeds up*, steadily, its velocity climbing in a straight line. That leftover
arrow is the whole story of how fast."

---

## SCENE 8 — The Full Picture + The Equation (1:56 – 2:10)
**Goal:** assemble the complete free-body diagram and name the result.
**Sees:** All arrows return to the block at once, fully labeled — **mg** (ghosted), **mg sinθ**, **mg
cosθ**, **N**, **f**, and the **net** — a complete free-body diagram. Beneath it, the equation writes
itself term by term, each term glowing as its arrow pulses: **a = g(sinθ − μcosθ)**.
- **Full free-body diagram** — every arrow on the block, labeled. IN 1:56 (all fade in together). STAYS
  to end.
- **Equation a = g(sinθ − μcosθ)** — below; each term reveals as its matching arrow pulses (sinθ↔mg sinθ,
  μcosθ↔friction). IN 1:59 (term by term). STAYS to end.
- **Callout** linking "μ" to the friction arrow: "μ = how grippy the surface is." IN 2:05. OUT 2:10.
**VO:** "Put it all together and you get the block's whole life on a ramp in one line: acceleration
equals g times sine theta, minus mu cosine theta. Steepness pushes it; friction holds it back; what's
left is how fast it goes."

---

## SCENE 9 — Ramps in the Real World (2:10 – 2:24)
**Goal:** why gentle ramps exist — the physics is practical.
**Sees:** Three quick vignettes share a row: a **loading ramp** (gentle, so the push needed — mg sinθ —
is small), a **wheelchair ramp** (shallow by design), and a **ski slope** (steep, for speed). Each shows
its θ and a tiny "slide force" gauge — small on the gentle ramps, large on the steep one.
- **Three ramp vignettes** — loading / wheelchair / ski, side by side, each with its angle + a slide-force
  gauge. IN 2:10 (stagger fade). OUT 2:22.
- **Label** "Gentle ramp = small sliding force = easy to control." IN 2:16. OUT 2:24.
**VO:** "This is why ramps are built the way they are. A gentle loading or wheelchair ramp keeps the
sliding force small — easy and safe. A ski slope does the opposite on purpose. Same equation, chosen
angle."

---

## SCENE 10 — Recap: One Block, Five Arrows (2:24 – 2:36)
**Goal:** lock the model in one clean frame.
**Sees:** Everything clears to the single block on its ramp with the five labeled arrows, and a compact
list lights up beside it: **① gravity down · ② split into slide + press · ③ normal cancels press · ④
friction fights slide · ⑤ what's left = acceleration.**
- **Block + 5 labeled arrows** — center. IN 2:24 (fade). STAYS to end.
- **Numbered recap list** — right, lights up ①–⑤ in time. IN 2:26 (stagger). STAYS to end.
- **Takeaway** "A ramp just splits gravity in two." — bottom. IN 2:32 (word-by-word). STAYS to 2:36.
**VO:** "So a ramp isn't complicated — it just splits gravity in two. One part slides, one part presses,
the surface cancels the press, friction fights the slide, and whatever's left decides how fast. Five
arrows, one block, every ramp."
