# Visual Script — "Why We Have Seasons"

**Format:** teacher-narrated animated explainer. **Runtime:** ~75s. **Frame:** 16:9, dark starfield
background throughout (deep navy-black, faint stars). **Recurring anchor:** the Sun stays on screen from
0:04 to 1:02 as the fixed reference; everything orbits or relates to it.

Reading the script: each scene lists **every on-screen object** with **IN** (appears) and **OUT**
(removed) timestamps, its position, and its motion. "VO" = teacher voice-over.

---

## SCENE 1 — The Question and the Trap (0:00 – 0:14)
**Claim to plant:** we all feel seasons — and the "obvious" reason is wrong.

**What the student sees:** A title fades in on black. The Sun appears center. A tiny Earth swings around
it on a slightly squashed orbit; when Earth passes closest to the Sun, a thought-label pops up — "Closer
= hotter?" — and a big red ✗ stamps over it.

**On-screen elements:**
- **Title text** "Why do we have seasons?" — centered, large. **IN 0:00** (fades up). **OUT 0:13** (fades
  down as scene ends).
- **Sun** — glowing yellow-orange sphere, screen center, soft radiating glow. **IN 0:04** (blooms from a
  point). **STAYS** (persists into Scenes 2–4).
- **Earth (small)** — blue-green dot, enters far right at 0:05, then **travels clockwise along a visibly
  elliptical orbit** around the Sun from **0:05 to 0:11**, passing near the Sun at the bottom of the loop.
  **OUT 0:13** (the orbit path and this small Earth fade; a bigger Earth takes over in Scene 2).
- **Orbit path** — faint dashed ellipse the small Earth rides. **IN 0:05**. **OUT 0:13**.
- **Label "Closer = hotter?"** — small caption in a pill, appears beside Earth at its closest point.
  **IN 0:08**. **OUT 0:13**.
- **Red ✗** — large, stamps over the label. **IN 0:11** (punchy scale-in). **OUT 0:13**.

**VO (0:00–0:13):** "Summer, winter — we all feel them. It's tempting to think summer is simply when
Earth is closest to the Sun. But that idea is wrong — and the real reason is more elegant."

---

## SCENE 2 — The Real Reason: The Tilt (0:14 – 0:32)
**Claim:** Earth spins on a tilted axis, and that tilt never changes direction.

**What the student sees:** The Sun slides to the left third. A large Earth appears on the right — big
enough to see detail. A bright axis line runs through it, clearly **tilted about 23.5° from vertical**.
The Earth **slowly spins** on that axis. A short curved arrow labels the tilt angle.

**On-screen elements:**
- **Sun** — (persisting) glides from center to the **left third** between 0:14–0:16, stays left.
- **Earth (large)** — blue-green globe with faint continents, enters **right-center** at 0:15 (fades in).
  It **rotates slowly in place** (spins on its axis) from 0:17 onward through the scene. **STAYS** into
  Scene 3.
- **Axis line (the pole)** — a glowing white line through Earth's center from top to bottom, **tilted
  23.5°** off vertical, with tiny "N" at the top end and "S" at the bottom. **IN 0:18** (draws on from
  center outward). **STAYS** into Scenes 3–4 (the tilt is the whole point).
- **Tilt-angle arc + label "23.5°"** — a small curved arrow between vertical and the axis, with the
  number. **IN 0:22**. **OUT 0:30** (fades once understood; the axis line remains).
- **Caption (bottom)** "Earth's axis is tilted — and always points the same way." **IN 0:24** (word by
  word). **OUT 0:31**.

**VO (0:14–0:31):** "Here's the key. Earth spins on an axis that's tilted about twenty-three and a half
degrees. And no matter where Earth is in its orbit, that axis keeps pointing the same direction in
space — like a spinning top that leans one way."

---

## SCENE 3 — Direct vs Slanted Light (0:32 – 0:52)
**Claim:** the tilt changes the *angle* sunlight hits the ground — direct light concentrates heat,
slanted light spreads it thin.

**What the student sees:** We zoom toward Earth. Parallel yellow sunlight rays stream in from the Sun on
the left. Split screen feeling: on the hemisphere leaning **toward** the Sun, rays hit nearly
straight-on and land in a tight, bright pool (labeled "summer — concentrated"). On the hemisphere
leaning **away**, the same rays strike at a shallow angle and smear across a much wider, dimmer patch
(labeled "winter — spread out").

**On-screen elements:**
- **Earth (large, tilted axis)** — (persisting) recenters slightly; axis still tilted. **STAYS**.
- **Sunlight rays** — a band of ~6 **parallel yellow arrows** traveling left→right from the Sun toward
  Earth. **IN 0:33** (stream on). **STAYS** to 0:50, then **OUT 0:51** (fade).
- **"Direct" hit zone (top hemisphere)** — where rays meet the hemisphere tilted toward the Sun: a
  **small, intense bright pool** on the surface. **IN 0:36**. **OUT 0:51**.
- **Label "Summer: direct rays, concentrated heat"** — pinned near the bright pool. **IN 0:38**.
  **OUT 0:51**.
- **"Slanted" hit zone (bottom hemisphere)** — where the same rays graze the hemisphere tilted away: a
  **wide, faint smear** of light across a bigger area. **IN 0:42**. **OUT 0:51**.
- **Label "Winter: slanted rays, heat spread thin"** — pinned near the smear. **IN 0:44**. **OUT 0:51**.
- **Comparison bracket** — a thin bracket contrasting the small bright pool vs the wide faint smear,
  with a tiny thermometer icon: full/red by the pool, low/blue by the smear. **IN 0:47**. **OUT 0:51**.

**VO (0:32–0:51):** "When your half of Earth leans toward the Sun, sunlight comes in almost straight
down — concentrated into a small area. That's summer. Lean away, and the same sunlight hits at a
shallow angle, smeared over a much wider patch. The heat is diluted. That's winter."

---

## SCENE 4 — Around the Year (0:52 – 1:08)
**Claim:** because the tilt stays fixed, the two hemispheres trade seasons as Earth orbits — and it's
never about distance.

**What the student sees:** Pull back out to the whole orbit. The Sun sits center. **Four Earths** are
shown at the four points of a near-circular orbit (June, September, December, March) — each keeping the
**same tilt direction**. At the June position the north pole leans toward the Sun (northern summer); at
December it leans away (northern winter). A small Earth then **glides once around the full orbit**, and a
"same distance" ruler flashes to kill the closeness myth.

**On-screen elements:**
- **Sun** — (persisting) recenters to screen center at 0:52. **OUT 1:02** (after this scene's point is
  made it can dim, but keep it lit until 1:02).
- **Near-circular orbit ring** — a clean faint circle around the Sun. **IN 0:53**. **STAYS** to 1:07.
- **Four Earths** at the 3-, 6-, 9-, 12-o'clock positions, each a small tilted globe, tilt axis all
  pointing the **same screen-direction** (say, up-right). **IN 0:54** (fade in one after another, ~0.3s
  apart). **STAY** to 1:06.
  - **Season tags** by each: "N. Summer" (top), "N. Autumn," "N. Winter" (bottom), "N. Spring." **IN
    0:57** (appear with their Earth). **OUT 1:06**.
- **Traveling Earth** — one highlighted Earth **glides smoothly around the full ring once** from 0:59 to
  1:04, its tilt fixed, visibly leaning toward the Sun at top and away at bottom. **OUT 1:06**.
- **"Same distance" ruler** — a double-headed measuring line from the Sun to the orbit, flashing equal
  length at the summer and winter positions, with a small "distance ≈ unchanged" note and a tiny ✗ over
  the earlier "closer = hotter" idea. **IN 1:02**. **OUT 1:06**.

**VO (0:52–1:06):** "Now watch the whole year. The tilt never changes direction — so as Earth orbits,
each hemisphere takes its turn leaning toward the Sun, then away. When it's summer up north, it's winter
down south, at the very same distance from the Sun. Distance was never the answer. The tilt is."

---

## SCENE 5 — Recap (1:08 – 1:18)
**Claim:** one sentence the student walks away with.

**What the student sees:** Everything clears to black except a single clean diagram: a tilted Earth, one
arrow of direct light (summer) and one arrow of slanted light (winter), and the takeaway line.

**On-screen elements:**
- **Everything from Scene 4** — **OUT 1:07** (fade to black).
- **Single tilted Earth (center)** — clean, axis tilted, gently spinning. **IN 1:08**. **STAYS** to end.
- **Two labeled rays** — one straight arrow hitting top ("direct = summer"), one shallow arrow grazing
  bottom ("slanted = winter"). **IN 1:10**. **STAY** to end.
- **Takeaway text (bottom)** "Seasons come from Earth's tilt — not its distance from the Sun." **IN
  1:12** (word by word). **STAYS** to end (1:18).

**VO (1:08–1:17):** "So remember: seasons happen because Earth is tilted — changing the angle of
sunlight — not because we move closer to the Sun."
