# Visual Script — "Why the Sky Is Blue (and Sunsets Red)"
~4:10 · theme: textbook (clean sky-and-light look) · recurring anchor: **a ray of sunlight** and the sky it paints

Core mechanism to make visible: **scattering** — tiny air molecules spray short (blue) light waves out in
all directions far more than long (red) ones. Everything the teacher says is on screen: white light, a
prism, molecules, a long red wave, a short blue wave, scatter arrows, the sky dome, the long sunset path.

---

## SCENE 1 — Same sun, two colors (0:00 – 0:22)
Goal: Pose the mystery — the *same* sky is blue by day and red at sunset. Why?
Sees: A deep-blue daytime sky fills the frame. The frame then splits and the right half becomes the same
sky at sunset — glowing orange-red over a horizon. A big "?" sits between the two halves.
Elements:
- `day-sky` (blue gradient panel) — IN 0:00 fills frame (fade); becomes the left half at 0:10; persists.
- `sun-day` (small bright disc, high) — IN 0:03; persists.
- `sunset-sky` (orange-red gradient) — IN 0:10 wipes in on the right half; persists.
- `sun-set` (large reddened disc near the horizon) — IN 0:13; persists.
- `q-mark` (big "?") — IN 0:18 center, between the halves (slam); OUT scene 2.
- `title` ("Same sun. Two colors.") — IN 0:19 top (word-by-word); OUT scene 2.
VO: [blue sky] "Look up on a clear day, and the sky is blue — a deep, endless blue, everywhere you look."
[sunset wipes in] "But watch that same sky at sunset, and it burns orange and red." [q-mark slams] "Same
sun. Same sky. So why do we see two completely different colors? The answer is hiding inside the light
itself."

## SCENE 2 — White light is every color (0:22 – 0:46)
Goal: Sunlight isn't "white" — it's every color layered together, from long red to short violet.
Sees: A single white beam leaves the sun, strikes a glass prism, and fans out the far side into a full
rainbow band — red at one end, violet at the other.
Elements:
- `sun-src` (bright disc, left) — IN 0:22; persists.
- `white-beam` (thick white ray, sun → prism) — IN 0:24 (draw); dims as the spectrum appears; persists faint.
- `prism` (glass triangle, center) — IN 0:27 (fade); persists.
- `spectrum` (rainbow band red→violet, fanning right of the prism) — IN 0:31 (wipe); persists.
- `ends` (labels "long red" / "short violet" at the band ends) — IN 0:38 (fade); OUT scene 3.
VO: [beam hits prism] "Start with one ray of sunlight. It looks plain and white." [spectrum fans out] "But
send it through a prism and it splits apart — because white light is really every color layered together."
[end labels] "Long, lazy red waves at one end… short, tight violet waves at the other. Hold onto that —
red is long, blue and violet are short. It's about to matter."

## SCENE 3 — The air isn't empty (0:46 – 1:08)
Goal: The "empty" air between you and space is actually crowded with molecules.
Sees: The camera pushes in from the wide sky into a small square of air, which fills with countless tiny
jiggling dots — molecules of nitrogen and oxygen.
Elements:
- `sky-patch` (pale panel with a shrinking zoom frame closing on it) — IN 0:46; persists.
- `molecules` (many small dots) — IN 0:50 scatter in across the frame (staggered fade); jiggle in place; persist.
- `air-label` ("nitrogen + oxygen") — IN 1:00 (fade); OUT scene 4.
VO: [zoom in] "Now, the air between you and space looks completely empty. It isn't." [molecules pop in] "Zoom
in far enough and it's packed — trillions of tiny molecules of nitrogen and oxygen, drifting everywhere."
[label] "And sunlight can't reach your eyes without crashing straight through all of them."

## SCENE 4 — Scattering: light sprays out (1:08 – 1:32)
Goal: When light hits a molecule it doesn't stop — it re-radiates in every direction. That's scattering.
Sees: A single light wave travels toward one lone molecule; on contact the molecule flashes and throws
little arrows of light outward in every direction at once.
Elements:
- `one-molecule` (single dot, center) — IN 1:08; flashes on impact; persists.
- `in-wave` (a wavy ray entering from the left) — IN 1:10 travels to the molecule (draw); OUT 1:20.
- `scatter-arrows` (arrows radiating outward in all directions) — IN 1:20 burst from the molecule (grow); pulse; OUT scene 5.
- `scatter-label` ("scattering") — IN 1:26 (fade); OUT scene 5.
VO: [wave approaches] "Here's the key moment — what happens when light meets one of those molecules."
[flash + arrows burst] "The molecule grabs the light for a split second, then flings it back out — in every
direction at once." [label] "That spray of light has a name: scattering. This one idea explains the whole
sky."

## SCENE 5 — Blue scatters far more than red (1:32 – 2:04)
Goal: Short (blue) waves scatter dramatically more than long (red) waves.
Sees: Two rays race side by side through a field of molecules — a long red wave and a short blue wave. The
red mostly sails straight through; the blue ricochets off molecule after molecule, spraying sideways. A
small graph in the corner shows scattering climbing steeply toward the short-wavelength (blue) end.
Elements:
- `molecule-field` (scattered dots across the frame) — IN 1:32; persist.
- `red-wave` (long-wavelength red ray) — IN 1:35 travels left→right nearly straight, one or two tiny nudges (move); persists to 1:56.
- `blue-wave` (short-wavelength blue ray) — IN 1:42 travels but bounces off many molecules, spraying sideways (move); persists to 1:56.
- `scatter-graph` (curve: scattering vs wavelength, steep rise toward blue) — IN 1:52 corner (draw); persists.
- `graph-call` (callout "blue ≈ 4× more") — IN 1:58 on the graph; OUT scene 6.
VO: [red wave slips through] "Send a long red wave into the crowd, and it mostly slips straight past —
barely bothered." [blue wave ricochets] "But a short blue wave slams into molecule after molecule and
sprays off in every direction." [graph draws] "The shorter the wave, the more it scatters — and not by a
little. Blue scatters about four times as much as red."

## SCENE 6 — So the whole sky glows blue (2:04 – 2:30)
Goal: Blue scattered out of the sunbeam arrives at your eye from every direction → the whole dome looks blue.
Sees: Wide view. The sun sits off in one corner and sends a broad beam across the sky; blue light sprays out
of the beam and floods the entire dome; a small figure on the ground looks up as blue rays arrive from all
directions.
Elements:
- `sun-corner` (disc, upper corner) — IN 2:04; persists.
- `sun-beam` (broad beam across the sky) — IN 2:06 (draw); persists faint.
- `blue-spray` (many blue scatter arrows filling the dome) — IN 2:10 (staggered grow); the sky panel flushes blue; persist.
- `observer` (small figure on the ground) — IN 2:18 (fade); persists.
- `eye-rays` (blue rays converging into the observer from all over the sky) — IN 2:22 (draw); OUT scene 7.
VO: [beam + blue spray fills dome] "Now pull all the way back. Sunlight pours across the sky, and all that
blue scatters out of the beam and bounces around the whole dome above you." [observer + converging rays]
"So when you look up — at any patch of sky — blue light is arriving from every direction at once. The entire
sky glows blue. There's your daytime answer."

## SCENE 7 — Then why not violet? (2:30 – 2:54)
Goal: Violet scatters even more, yet we see blue — because the sun emits less violet and our eyes are weak to it.
Sees: A quick aside. The spectrum band returns; the violet end flashes "scatters even MORE." Then two small
icons: a sun emitting little violet, and an eye-sensitivity curve that dips at violet. An arrow lands on blue.
Elements:
- `mini-spectrum` (the rainbow band again) — IN 2:30; violet end highlighted; OUT 2:52.
- `violet-note` ("violet scatters even more!") — IN 2:34 (fade); OUT 2:48.
- `sun-icon` (sun + "emits less violet") — IN 2:40 (fade); OUT 2:52.
- `eye-icon` (eye + "eyes weak to violet") — IN 2:44 (fade); OUT 2:52.
- `result` ("→ we see BLUE") — IN 2:50 (slam); OUT scene 8.
VO: [violet flashes] "Quick puzzle — violet waves are even shorter than blue, so they scatter even more.
Shouldn't the sky be violet?" [two icons] "Two reasons it isn't: the sun sends out less violet to begin
with, and our eyes are far less sensitive to it." [result] "So the color that wins — the one that floods the
sky — is blue."

## SCENE 8 — Sunset: a much longer path (2:54 – 3:20)
Goal: At sunset the sun's light travels through far more atmosphere to reach you.
Sees: A cross-section of Earth's curve with a thin atmosphere shell above it. Midday: the sun is overhead and
its light takes a short, straight path down to an observer. Sunset: the sun sits on the horizon and its light
skims sideways through a long stretch of the shell. The two paths are compared — the sunset one far longer.
Elements:
- `earth-curve` (arc) + `atmo-shell` (thin band above it) — IN 2:54; persist.
- `observer2` (figure standing on the curve) — IN 2:56; persists.
- `day-path` (short ray straight down from an overhead sun) — IN 3:00 (draw); label "midday — short path"; ghosts at 3:10.
- `set-path` (long ray skimming through the shell from a horizon sun) — IN 3:08 (draw, long); label "sunset — long path"; persists.
- `path-brace` (bracket spanning the long path) — IN 3:16; OUT scene 9.
VO: [midday path] "So why does sunset flip to red? It comes down to how far the light has to travel through
the air." [short path] "At noon the sun is overhead — a short, direct path straight down to you." [long
sunset path draws] "But at sunset the sun sits right on the horizon, and its light skims sideways through a
huge, long stretch of atmosphere before it ever reaches your eyes."

## SCENE 9 — Red is what's left (3:20 – 3:48)
Goal: Over that long path all the blue scatters away; only red/orange survives → red sun and sky.
Sees: Follow the long sunset ray. As it crosses the long atmosphere, blue keeps peeling off to the sides and
draining away, and the ray shifts from white toward orange and then red, until only red light continues
straight to the observer. The sun and horizon flush deep orange-red.
Elements:
- `long-ray` (ray starting white/full-color at the horizon sun) — IN 3:20 travels toward the observer.
- `blue-leak` (blue arrows peeling off the ray all along its path) — IN 3:24 (staggered); the ray recolors white→orange→red as it loses blue; OUT 3:38.
- `red-arrival` (red ray reaching the observer) — IN 3:38 (draw); persists.
- `sunset-glow` (sky and sun flush deep red) — IN 3:40 (fade); persists.
- `red-cap` ("blue scattered away → red is left") — IN 3:44; OUT scene 10.
VO: [blue peels off] "Across that long journey, the blue scatters away long before it reaches you — bounced
off into other parts of the sky." [ray reddens] "What's left to travel straight through is the
long-wavelength light: orange and red." [glow] "And so the setting sun, and the whole sky around it, blaze
red. Same scattering as the noon sky — just a much longer path."

## SCENE 10 — One rule, two skies (3:48 – 4:10)
Goal: Tie it together: one scattering rule paints both the blue day and the red sunset.
Sees: A clean chain lights up in sequence — white sunlight → a sky full of tiny molecules → it branches:
(short path) blue scatters everywhere = BLUE SKY; (long sunset path) blue is drained, red remains = RED
SUNSET. The anchor sun sits in the middle.
Elements:
- `chain-white` ("white sunlight = all colors") — IN 3:48 (fade).
- `chain-mol` ("tiny molecules scatter short waves most") — IN 3:52 (fade); arrow from white light.
- `blue-branch` ("day · short path · blue scatters → BLUE SKY") — IN 3:56 (fade), tinted blue.
- `red-branch` ("sunset · long path · blue lost → RED SKY") — IN 4:00 (fade), tinted red.
- `takeaway` ("One rule of scattering paints both skies.") — IN 4:05 bottom (word-by-word); persists.
VO: [chain builds] "So here's the whole picture. White sunlight, full of colors, meets a sky full of tiny
molecules." [blue branch] "By day, the short blue waves scatter across the entire dome — and you get a blue
sky." [red branch] "At sunset, the light's long path scatters that blue away and leaves the red behind."
[takeaway] "One simple rule — small things scatter short waves the most — paints both the blue of noon and
the red of evening."
