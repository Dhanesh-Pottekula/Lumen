# Visual Script — "The Roman Empire: Rise and Fall"

**Runtime:** ~2:40. **Background:** aged-parchment map tone (a history lesson lives on a map + a
timeline). **Recurring anchors:** a **map of the Mediterranean** (the empire grows and shrinks on it
across the lesson) and a **timeline** of a thousand years (753 BC → 476 AD) we return to. **Voice:** a
storyteller-teacher — the drama of how one city ruled the world, and why it couldn't last.

Every object lists **IN** (appear · where · how), behavior, **OUT** (removed · how). Nothing lingers.

---

## SCENE 1 — One City, a Thousand Years (0:00 – 0:12)
**Goal:** the astonishing claim that hooks the whole lesson.
**Sees:** A single dot glows on a map of Italy — Rome. The word **SPQR** fades in. Then a **thousand-year
span** streaks across the bottom (753 BC → 476 AD).
- **Med map (dim)** — the Mediterranean, faint. IN 0:00 (fade). STAYS (the anchor).
- **Rome dot** — a glowing marker on Italy. IN 0:03 (iris). STAYS to 0:12.
- **Title** "From one city to the whole world" — center. IN 0:05 (word-by-word). OUT 0:12.
- **Span bar** "753 BC — 476 AD · 1000 years" — bottom. IN 0:09. OUT 0:12.
**VO:** "One city, on seven hills by a river, grew to rule the entire Mediterranean world — and held it
for a thousand years. This is how Rome rose, and why it fell."

---

## SCENE 2 — Kings, Then a Republic (0:12 – 0:30)
**Goal:** Rome's first big idea — get rid of the king, share power.
**Sees:** Split screen. LEFT: a lone **crown** (one king, 753–509 BC). RIGHT: it's crossed out and
replaced by **two consuls + a Senate** (many figures) — power shared, elected, checked. A one-year term
and "SPQR = the Senate and People of Rome" labels it.
- **Kingdom panel (crown)** — left. IN 0:14 (fade). OUT 0:29.
- **Republic panel (two consuls + senate rows)** — right. IN 0:18 (build up). OUT 0:29.
- **Red ✗ over the crown** — IN 0:16. OUT 0:29.
- **Label** "509 BC: the Republic — power shared, not owned." — footer. IN 0:22. OUT 0:30.
**VO:** "For its first centuries Rome had kings. Then, in 509 BC, they threw the last one out and built a
republic — two consuls elected each year, checked by a Senate. No one man would own Rome. That idea made
it strong."

---

## SCENE 3 — The Republic Conquers the Sea (0:30 – 0:50)
**Goal:** show expansion — Italy, then the Mediterranean, then the great rival Carthage.
**Sees:** The map takes over. The Roman region **grows**: first all of **Italy**, then across the sea.
Then a rival glows in North Africa — **Carthage**. **Arrows clash** between them (the Punic Wars), and
Carthage is erased; Rome's territory floods across the western Mediterranean.
- **Med map (bright)** — full. IN 0:30. STAYS to 0:50.
- **Rome region (grows)** — Italy → western Med, animating. IN 0:32 (grows over 0:32–0:44). STAYS.
- **Carthage marker** — glowing in N. Africa. IN 0:36. OUT 0:46 (fades as it's defeated).
- **War arrows (Rome ↔ Carthage)** — clash across the sea. IN 0:40. OUT 0:46.
- **Label** "264–146 BC: the Punic Wars — Rome beats Carthage." IN 0:42. OUT 0:50.
**VO:** "The republic went to war — and rarely stopped. It took all of Italy, then collided with
Carthage, the great sea-power of the west. Three brutal wars later, Carthage was gone, and the western
Mediterranean was Roman."

---

## SCENE 4 — The Machine of Conquest (0:50 – 1:10)
**Goal:** WHY Rome won — the legion, the roads, the engineering.
**Sees:** Three stacked reveals. First a **legion** — rows of soldiers in a tight formation (discipline).
Then a **road network** draws itself across the map (fast movement). Then an **aqueduct arch** rises
(engineering, supply). Each is labeled as a reason Rome outlasted everyone.
- **Legion formation** — a grid of soldier icons, left. IN 0:52 (build). OUT 1:08.
- **"Discipline" label** — by the legion. IN 0:54. OUT 1:08.
- **Road lines** — straight roads drawing across a mini-map, center. IN 0:58 (draw). OUT 1:08.
- **"Roads: move armies fast" label** — IN 1:00. OUT 1:08.
- **Aqueduct arches** — rising, right. IN 1:03 (draw). OUT 1:08.
- **"Engineering + supply" label** — IN 1:05. OUT 1:08.
**VO:** "Why Rome and not someone else? Three things. Legions — professional, drilled, relentless. Roads
— fifty thousand miles of them, so armies and orders moved faster than anyone's. And engineering — walls,
aqueducts, supply — that let Rome hold what it took."

---

## SCENE 5 — The Republic Tears Itself Apart (1:10 – 1:28)
**Goal:** success breaks the republic — generals grow bigger than the state.
**Sees:** The shared-power balance from Scene 2 returns — but now **one figure (a general) swells** until
it dwarfs the Senate. Civil-war arrows flare. A name lands: **Julius Caesar**, crossing a river (the
Rubicon), then a dagger — assassination, 44 BC. The republic is cracking.
- **Senate rows (small)** — center. IN 1:12. OUT 1:26.
- **General figure (grows huge)** — swells over the Senate. IN 1:14 (grows). OUT 1:26.
- **"Caesar crosses the Rubicon, 49 BC" label** — IN 1:18. OUT 1:26.
- **Dagger + "44 BC: Caesar killed"** — IN 1:22 (slam). OUT 1:28.
**VO:** "But conquest made generals richer and more powerful than the state itself. Ambition tore the
republic apart in civil war. Julius Caesar seized control — and was stabbed to death by senators trying
to save the old republic. They were too late."

---

## SCENE 6 — Augustus: One Man, Many Masks (1:28 – 1:46)
**Goal:** the republic becomes an empire — quietly.
**Sees:** From Caesar's death, a young heir — **Augustus** — steps forward. He keeps the *look* of the
republic (Senate still there, faded) but holds the *real* power himself (a laurel crown settles). A
comparison bar: **Republic (power shared)** dims; **Empire (power held)** brightens. 27 BC.
- **Augustus figure + laurel** — center. IN 1:30 (fade). OUT 1:45.
- **Ghost Senate (faded behind)** — IN 1:32. OUT 1:45.
- **Republic→Empire comparison bar** — shared → held. IN 1:36. OUT 1:45.
- **Label** "27 BC: Augustus — Rome becomes an empire." IN 1:40. OUT 1:46.
**VO:** "His heir, Augustus, learned the lesson. He kept the Senate, the titles, the *appearance* of the
republic — while quietly holding all real power himself. In 27 BC, without ever calling himself king,
Augustus became Rome's first emperor."

---

## SCENE 7 — The Peak: Pax Romana (1:46 – 2:04)
**Goal:** the height — greatest extent, peace, scale.
**Sees:** The map **floods to its greatest extent** (117 AD, Trajan) — Britain to the Persian Gulf, the
Rhine to the Sahara. Big counters roll up: **~5,000,000 km²**, **~60 million people**, **~200 years of
relative peace (Pax Romana)**. Trade flows pulse along the roads and sea lanes.
- **Empire region (floods to peak)** — the map fills. IN 1:46 (grows). STAYS to 2:04.
- **Extent stat "≈5,000,000 km²"** — IN 1:50 (count up). OUT 2:04.
- **Population stat "≈60,000,000 people"** — IN 1:54 (count up). OUT 2:04.
- **Trade flows** — pulsing along roads/sea. IN 1:58. OUT 2:04.
- **"Pax Romana · ~200 years of peace" label** — IN 2:00. OUT 2:04.
**VO:** "By 117 AD Rome reached its greatest size — five million square kilometres, sixty million people,
a fifth of humanity, under one law and one coin. For two centuries the Mediterranean knew relative peace:
the Pax Romana. It felt eternal."

---

## SCENE 8 — A Thousand Years on One Line (2:04 – 2:20)
**Goal:** anchor the whole story in time before we explain the fall.
**Sees:** A full **timeline** unrolls, 753 BC → 476 AD, with **three era bands** — Kingdom, Republic,
Empire — and the key events we've seen (founding, republic, Punic Wars, Caesar, Augustus, peak). A
**playhead sweeps** from start to the peak, then pauses — the empire is now as big as it will ever be.
- **Timeline (eras + events)** — full width. IN 2:04 (draw). STAYS to 2:20.
- **Playhead** — sweeps 753 BC → 117 AD, pauses. IN 2:08 (sweeps). STAYS to 2:20.
- **"Peak — now what stops it?" label** — IN 2:16. OUT 2:20.
**VO:** "A thousand years on one line: centuries of kings, five centuries of republic, then the empire —
climbing all the way to that peak. And then, slowly, it stops climbing. So what brings a thousand-year
empire down?"

---

## SCENE 9 — Why It Fell: Not One Reason, Many (2:20 – 2:40)
**Goal:** the fall was a stack of causes, not a single blow.
**Sees:** A **bar chart / stacked pressures** builds: **overexpansion** (too big to defend),
**economic strain** (inflation, taxes), **political chaos** (emperors murdered, ~20 in 50 years),
**invasions** (arrows pressing on every border). Each bar rises as it's named — the weight piling up.
- **Pressures bar chart** — overexpansion / economy / instability / invasions. IN 2:22 (bars rise
  one by one). STAYS to 2:38.
- **Border-pressure arrows on a mini-map** — pushing inward. IN 2:30. OUT 2:38.
- **"No single cause — the weight piled up" label** — IN 2:34. OUT 2:40.
**VO:** "There was no single blow. The empire grew too big to defend. Money weakened — coins debased,
taxes crushing. Emperors were made and murdered by the army, twenty in fifty years. And on every
frontier, migrating peoples pushed in. The weight simply piled up."

---

## SCENE 10 — The Split and the Fall (2:40 – 2:58)
**Goal:** divide to survive; the West falls, the East endures.
**Sees:** The map **splits down the middle** into **West** and **East** (285 AD). The **West** darkens,
its borders collapse inward, and **476 AD** stamps on Rome — the last western emperor deposed. But the
**East glows on** from Constantinople — the Byzantine Empire, another **1,000 years**.
- **Dividing line (West | East)** — down the map. IN 2:42 (draw). STAYS to 2:58.
- **West region (collapses)** — darkens, borders cave in. IN 2:46 (shrinks). OUT 2:56.
- **"476 AD: the West falls" stamp** — IN 2:50 (slam). OUT 2:58.
- **East region (glows)** — Constantinople bright, persists. IN 2:52. STAYS to 2:58.
**VO:** "To survive, Rome split in two. The western half, overrun, finally gave way — in 476 the last
western emperor was deposed, and the Rome of the west was gone. But the eastern half, ruled from
Constantinople, lived on as the Byzantine Empire for another thousand years."

---

## SCENE 11 — What Rome Left Behind (2:58 – 3:12)
**Goal:** the empire fell, but it's still all around us.
**Sees:** Four icons light up around the fading map: **law** (a scroll — courts, rights), **language**
(letters — Latin → Spanish, French, Italian…), **roads & engineering** (an arch), **the calendar**
(months named for Caesar and Augustus). Rome dissolves, but its outlines remain in the modern world.
- **Legacy icons (law · language · roads · calendar)** — around the map, light up in turn. IN 3:00
  (stagger). STAYS to 3:12.
- **"Rome fell — its ideas didn't" label** — IN 3:06. OUT 3:12.
**VO:** "Rome the empire ended. But Roman law underlies courts today. Latin became Spanish, French,
Italian, and half of English. Its roads and domes still stand. Even our calendar carries the names of
Caesar and Augustus. The empire fell — its ideas never did."

---

## SCENE 12 — Recap: The Arc (3:12 – 3:24)
**Goal:** the whole thousand-year arc in one breath.
**Sees:** The timeline returns, and a single line lights up its shape: **city → republic → conquest →
empire → peak → overreach → split → fall → legacy.** A last takeaway settles.
- **Timeline (arc highlighted)** — IN 3:12. STAYS to end.
- **Arc labels lighting up** — city→…→legacy. IN 3:14 (stagger). STAYS.
- **Takeaway** "Rome rose by sharing power, and fell when it couldn't share it back." — IN 3:20
  (word-by-word). STAYS to 3:24.
**VO:** "A city that shared power grew into an empire that couldn't. It rose on discipline, roads, and
law — and fell under its own weight. A thousand years, from a village on the Tiber to the world, and
back. That is the story of Rome."
